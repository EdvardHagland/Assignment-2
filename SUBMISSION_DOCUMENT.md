Assignment 2 Project Note

In this assignment I wanted to map the semantic structure of consultation responses: what recurring frames appear when the corpus is not pre-tagged. I built the pipeline on top of Assignment 1, where I scraped and normalized the consultation material and preserved attachment formatting in md format. That earlier step proved useful as many of the substantive submissions could now easily be chunked on real blank-line breaks instead of being reconstructed from flattened text. The pipeline itself is generic. It can consume any compiled consultation file, turn it into analysis units, embed those units, cluster them, write inspection tables, and optionally pass a structured evidence bundle to Gemini.

The main design choice is the unit of analysis. I do not embed whole responses by default. In prepare_corpus.py I use attachment-aware paragraph chunking when attachment markdown exists, with a sentence-window fallback when it does not. In the current pipeline this gave better thematic resolution than document-level embeddings, but it also produced the main methodological vulnerability in the project: the distribution of analysis units is not neutral. Long institutional submissions generate many more units than short citizen replies, which can give a small number of detailed submissions disproportionate weight in the semantic space. The method is also vulnerable when a consultation contains citizen mobilization at scale with many coordinated or near-duplicate submissions. In those cases the dominant citizen pattern can saturate the semantic space and suppress less frequent viewpoints. This was especially visible in the consultation on trade in seal products, where minority positions, from both institutions and individuals, became harder to recover in the current pipeline.

My first runs on a laptop used smaller embedding models and the result was mostly noise. I moved a heavier run to Google Colab and switched to BAAI/bge-m3, used in dense SentenceTransformer mode. That was a better fit because the model is documented as multilingual, supports more than 100 languages, and is designed for inputs ranging from short passages to documents of up to 8,192 tokens. In my code, I only use the dense embedding interface, not the sparse or multi-vector features. The hard part of this exercise was the granularity, weighting, and interpretation. I kept an R reporting layer, including ggplot-style figures and PDF/report compilation. Much of that could probably have stayed in Python, but the mixed R/Python setup matched the course requirement. I used Codex heavily to accelerate the build.

In the quantitative stage I embed the prepared units, normalize the vectors, reduce them with PCA, and search across a small range of KMeans solutions instead of fixing one cluster count in advance. I also wrote a separate validation layer with Hopkins, permutation null checks, bootstrap stability, and cross-algorithm comparison. I theorised that statistical tests would be possible to differentiate the clusters from each other, with something akin to a P-value. Since random scatterplots should be easy to model. In practice I always ran with skip-validation because that stage is CPU-bound and too slow for iterative work. I am unsure if this is a practice that is used for separating "real" clusters from artifacts of the algorithm, which will find clusters. 

The qualitative stage is my addition to the assignment. The code sends a structured bundle containing cluster statistics, lexical tables, metadata signals, overlap summaries, and what I call representative evidence. That evidence is selected automatically: a few examples close to the cluster centroid are taken as the core, and a few more varied examples are added to show internal spread. This is better than sending only one central excerpt, but it can still misrepresent a mixed cluster, especially when long professional or coordinated responses dominate the local space. I therefore do not think ‘representative evidence’ is a neutral label. At the same time, Gemini is not being asked for a loose qualitative essay. The prompt is deliberately strict, comparative, and grounded in the bundled tables, and in stronger qualitative runs I overrode the default and used Gemini 3.1 Pro.

Four bundled corpora are included in the repository: 12527 Requirements for Artificial Intelligence, 16213 European Open Digital Ecosystems, 14031 Fitness check of EU legislation on trade in seal products, and 1424 European Defence Fund and EU Defence Industrial Development Programme. The exemplar corpus is 12527. Although it is smaller in number of submissions than some of the other bundled corpora, paragraph-level chunking still yields several thousand analysis units, which is sufficient for exploratory embedding and clustering. Several of the other bundled corpora are more heavily shaped by mobilization campaigns, and with the current chunk-level method those repeated submissions tend to saturate the semantic space and suppress less frequent viewpoints.

This pipeline is useful as exploratory infrastructure, but it is not the best final tool for qualitative understanding. If the goal is to surface emergent semantic neighborhoods in a consultation corpus, it is useful. If the goal is to show who said what, under which category, and with clear traceability, my earlier category-first consultation project is better. (which I shared with you at ed-tech.no) That system is more useful for qualitative interpretation. For understanding consultation corpora, in future work, I would continue developing the ed-tech.no solution for better qualitative analysis.
